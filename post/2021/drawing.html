Drawing

2021–06–15 ~900 words

Drawing has been the most efficient way to communicate visual ideas for centuries. From traditional instruments such as charcoal, to modern digital drawing software, the simple act of toning a surface with media to approximate the visual world is critical from visual arts, architecture, design, to engineering.

In distinction to painting, drawing involves dry media, with pastels bridging the gap towards the liquid media, like acrylic and oil paints. Drawing with its minimal equipment required to produce meaningful work, more so than painting, enables artistic expression within every milieu.

Detour: Vertigo (1958)

Alfred Hitchcock, depicted in the accompaning drawing, is someone I first encountered by reading the books and listening to the taped versions of the popular juvenile detective book series “Alfred Hitchcock and the Three Investigators” as a child, or rather their German translation.[1] Years later, my interest in film swiftly brought be back to the “Master of Suspense”. Hitchcock’s Vertigo is not only objectively one of the best movies, but coincidentally also one of my personal favourites.

Retired, acrophobic detective Scottie (James Stewart) is asked by Gavin Elster, an old friend, to follow his wife named Madeleine Elster (Kim Novak), as she seems to strangely identify herself with Carlotta, a relative of hers who commited suicide. Scottie and Madeleine fall in love, but as she too commits suicide by jumping of a mission’s tower, Scottie gets depressed, descents into madness and tries to find Madeleine by frequenting places she has been to, noticing a woman named Judy. The viewer learns that this woman is “Madeleine” and was hired to act as Gavin’s wife to cover-up a murder, exploiting Scotties fear of heights. Scottie keeps wanting Judy to resemble Madeleine, but soon notices her necklace that is also shown on a painting of Carlotta, uncovering the scheme. He wants to drive her back to the alledged suicide scene, where he then finally overcomes his vertigo. As the two admit their love for each other, the story ends tragically as Judy falls to her death by accident.

This story leaves us with parallels between the characters; while at first glance, Scottie seems to be the victim of a murder cover-up scheme initiated by Gavin Elster and Judy, Scottie progressively becomes similar to Gavin, as he too objectifies Judy in order to realize his dream, oblivious to her infatuation for him. It would be easy to sympathize with Kim Novak’s Judy, as the sport of misfortune she is, tragically falling in love with a man who longs only for a woman that has never been real, then later falling to her own death; but didn’t she have it coming by tricking Scottie and taking money to cover up a crime?

Hitchcock’s female lead actors usually do not radiate this pleasant ambiguity, making Vertigo suspenseful by letting the viewer feel uncomfortable regardless of their own interpretation, or allegiance.

Now let’s get back to the point.1

Drawing of Alfred Hitchcock

The reason for this motive is not that I like Vertigo so much, in reality I just wanted to draw an older man with wrinkles and many opportunities to introduce sharp detail in order to conveniently create a realistic look. While I value the craftsmanship aspects of drawing, I do not wish to lose all artistic interpretation.

This is a list of products I use for drawings:

  • Copic marker, N6 Neutral Grey
    • for giving the clothes an even background color; alcohol-based ink colours
  • Pentel GraphGear 1000, 0.5mm
    • primary detail mechanical pencil
  • uni POSCA PC-1MR, white
    • water-based paint marker for highlights
  • Brunnen ART ERASER
    • reliably removes charcoal and lightens areas without streaks
  • edding 780 paint marker 0.8mm, white
    • highlights; alternative to POSCA markers
  • Tombow MONO zero
    • primary detail eraser; for creating hair highlights
  • Faber-Castell Ecco Pigment, 0.05
    • fine liner for detail, darkest blacks
  • Faber-Castell Polychromos, white
    • highlights; did not work well, as it isn’t ink based
  • Faber-Castell 9000 – 4H, 2H, HB, 3B, 6B, 8B
    • primary pencil range, used 6B the most
  • Faber-Castell Perfection 7057
    • fine eraser; alternative to Tombow MONO zero
  • Stabilo point 88 fine 0.4, black
    • dark lines; alternative to Faber-Castell Ecco Pigment
  • rolled papyrus pencil smudger
    • smooth transitions
  • heavy, but fine drawing paper
    • not too thin to disintegrate during erasing, but fine as to not have visible surface texture
  • Pigma Micron
    • not sure if I used them for this, but they are my favorite pens

As can be seen in the list above, I started to incorporate ink based products, mainly to achieve higher contrast. Most high-quality ink pens and markers are manufactured in Japan by brands—often with comic book artists in mind—like Sakura Color Products, Tombow, Uni Mitsubishi Pencil, Copic and Pentel, known for popularizing the Fudepen. This certainly improved my drawings, although it technically means that the “artworks” are a mixture of drawing and painting, if we take the definitions of the first paragraph as gospel.

In retrospect I should have toned the background in a light grey, that would improve the drawing immensely. To some extent I enjoy seeing mistakes, or wrong decisions in my drawings, because only then do I have a chance to improve.
