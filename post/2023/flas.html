Foreign Language Area Studies

2023–01–18 ~1000 words

Since the 1970s, Foreign Language and Area Studies (Flas) scholarships have been funded through the US’ National Defense Education Act. This post attempts an axiological discussion of area studies, second language acquisition and the interdependence of these prima facie disjoined subject areas.

By introducing this topic, I am expanding the established network of thought presented on this website, to further interlink seemingly independent subject areas, as this often leads to innovative ideas.

The Case for Area Studies

A promising way to link previous considerations of art and sports to area studies is to look at their common evaluative aspect. Axiology connects aesthetics with area studies via ethics when introducing area studies via Rawls’ Theory of Justice. Ethics addresses questions of morality, which in turn assigns a value to human behavior. This assignment is according to moral values and norms that are, as I propose defying normative ethic’s contestations, culturally and temporally relative; defined by a group, society or culture. Good moral character is considered most beneficial to society.

Rawls states that “character can be molded by antecedent circumstances” and people “acquire a desire to act justly, and to do so for the right reasons, when they have lived under and benefited from just institutions”.[1] A just society causes its constituting citizens to flourish, it induces existential meaning and an interest to act morally; in that sense character is subject to moral luck. A trait that helps to propel this feedback mechanism is a virtue, such as participating in the civil public’s deliberations to weigh interests not your own. Area studies offer a pure form to exercise this virtue and add to a moral character and just society via the taken civic responsibility. By the holistic engagement with other cultures and societies within the global landscape one creates and fosters a set of virtues for a transareal citizen.[2]

Area studies as a discipline has been criticized for being too specific and detailed without scientific concept building or generalizing; merely telling stories by accidentally stumbling into the examined regions’ otherness. This putative weakness, a researcher in “Rebirth of Area Studies” argues, is a strength since “the fact that we not only study ‘otherness’ but often find ourselves through our travels and our readings in foreign languages to be an ‘other’”.[3] As I hypothesized above, this engagement could be conducive to good character development; second language acquisition in particular is known to increase “tolerance of ambiguity”.[4]

Second Language Acquisition

Communication by language is a form of information transmission used to transfer thoughts. Speech norms are thus a necessary condition for cultural exchange. The so called Sapir–Whorf hypothesis further states, that at least linguistic relativity is a given and influences of language feed back into thought creation, hence second language acquisition (Sla) is a necessary condition for fully understanding a foreign culture with a distinct language.

Linguistic theories about language learnability and acquisition can be divided based on their stance regarding linguistic nativism. Neurobiologically minded approaches conclude that, “at this time there is no convincing evidence for domain-specific circuitry for language.”, hence speaking for general nativism and putting a damper on Noam Chomsky’s Universal Grammar Theory.[5] During Self-regulated learning (Srl), setting goals and choosing suitable learning techniques is elementary, the foci of second language learners should thus be geared towards applied linguistics’ empirically verified language learning techniques instead of more general, philosophical hypothesis, if the goal is efficient L2 acquisition.

The declarative/procedural model supports both general nativism, as well as the general approach taken by linguist Stephen Krashen, because it has been shown than “learning to identify relatively simple parts of language, such as words, is facilitated by effortful learning, whereas learning more complex aspects of language, such as grammatical features, is impeded by effortful learning”.[6] The former equates to declarative memory, learning of language morphology—word roots, affixes, conjugation, etc.—mentioned in the latter would be helped with procedural memory system usage. Both immersion1 and deliberate practice[8] have their place.

Stephen Krashen’s approach focuses on compelling, comprehensible input in form of free voluntary reading [9] —meaning the extensive type— that is effective when around 98% of vocabulary in the input text is known.[10] The spaced repetition learning approach is based on the spacing and testing effect studied in psychology. Long-term potentiation in neuroscience denotes a type of persistently amplified signaling between neural circuits, and is cited as an underlying factor for the validity of the mentioned phenomena.[11] Spaced repetition is what active recall over increasing time intervals is called, and is a promising optimization of memory capability.[12] [13] The mental work done during active recall is most effectively used when one is just about to forget what one has learned—this is usually the case with increasing time intervals.[14] Spaced repetition software (Srs) has been in development since the 1980, to implement an optimal utilization of this effect. In 1987, Piotr Wozniak implemented the first algorithm for computer-based flashcards with SM-2, which modern popular options like anki are largely based on.[15] [16] Since the exact forgetting intervals can at most be approximated stochastically, an adaptation of these must be transmitted by the user with reference to the concrete remembering success for each card; pell-mell execution will not work, deliberate practice is imperative.

Flashcards based on a frequency dictionary’s word list derived from an appropriate corpus assures targeted initial vocabulary acquisition. For initial grammar acquisition, reading through frequency sequenced sentences exhibiting different grammatical structures, like example sentences from a grammar workbook is helpful. As a next step up from corpora based on subtitles, that belong to the Subtlex movement and are more aligned with spoken language than the traditional text based corpora, tools like Subs2Srs can be utilized to generate flashcards of sentences from subtitled content.[17] Epub formatted eBooks too can manually, or rather likely via regular expressions, be converted into Srs readable format, paving the way towards extended reading. Generating narration from these book passages with Tts engines, like coqui-tts for python, could assist focus while reading and improve listening comprehension. Interactive conversation with large language models (Llm) and usage of neural network based translators will likely amount to a paradigm shift in how and for what reason languages are learned in the near future. I imagine Sla will be less important for communication, and become even more about virtue signalling like sports competitions.

The significant interplay between target language and its associated culture has been laid out above via Rawls and area studies.
